Harrow the Ninth

January 1st, 2012

Written by Hannah Vadakken

I’ve tried to keep my reviews focused on first books as an introduction to romantasy, but Harrow the Ninth is my one exception. I’ll avoid major spoilers, but if you enjoyed Gideon the Ninth, it’s best to go in blind.

It is impossible to talk about Harrow without first talking about the first book in the series, Gideon. Gideon the Ninth follows Gideon Nav, a sarcastic swordswoman forced to aid necromancer Harrowhark Nonagesimus in a deadly trial set by God. Trapped in a decaying palace with rival necromancers, the pair must solve a murder mystery while mastering a dangerous magic system, developing a reluctant partnership that turns into something more.

In case you want a more in-depth recap (or spoilers, I won’t judge), you can check out Recaptain’s plot summary.

When I first read Gideon, I felt it didn’t fully capitalize on its potential. It sets up a fascinating sci-fi/fantasy world with necromancers, interplanetary travel, goth bone witch cultists, and a unique magic system, but Muir limits the story to a relatively simple murder mystery within a single castle. This constraint, paired with Gideon’s perspective as a non-magical jock who is more occupied with her sword and impressing the ladies, keeps the story grounded, but it also makes the world feel smaller than it really is. While I still enjoyed the book, especially its humor and character dynamics, it left me wanting a more ambitious exploration of the setting.

Harrow the Ninth, which follows Harrow’s trials and travels as a lyctor working directly for God on the Mithraeum, does exactly that. Where Gideon is straightforward, Harrow is chaotic and complex, throwing the reader into a fractured narrative shaped by an unreliable protagonist. In Harrow, Harrowhark is, and I promise I am not exaggerating, schizophrenic, haunted, amnesiac, lobotomized, and being actively lied to by her “allies”, all at the same time. All of this works to create a disorienting but incredibly compelling reading experience. Harrow also fully takes off the training wheels in regards to the world and magic system and delivers a galactic, necromantic drama filled with shifting perspectives, mysteries, and constant uncertainty. Despite this complexity, the twists rarely feel unearned. They are carefully set up and reward attentive reading while not being so foreshadowed as to feel frustratingly obvious.

That said, the book isn’t without flaws. Some of the humor, particularly the more modern internet references, can break immersion at key moments. I understand their relevance, but Gideon cracking a Miette reference when going to kill her mother just made me groan, and John’s “None pizza with left grief” joke actually made me put the book down for a minute. The use of first and second-person narration also felt quite gimmicky to me, especially since it was obvious who the second narrator was from page one and such perspectives were never present in Gideon. Additionally, Muir’s prose can just generally come across as awkward at times, pulling the reader out of otherwise strong scenes.

Even with these issues, Harrow the Ninth stands out for its ambition and willingness to challenge its audience. It expands the world in incredible ways, continues Gideon’s trend of incredible and hilarious character dynamics despite the significantly darker plot, and hauntingly (pun intended) depicts Harrow’s struggles with identity, loss, and religion. While it may not work for everyone, it’s a bold, innovative sequel that builds on the set-up of Gideon and rewards readers willing to engage with its complexity.

Hasbrouck Heights Public Library

January 1st, 2012

Written by Hannah Vadakken

As a lifelong Hasbrouck Heights resident, the Hasbrouck Heights Public Library has been a constant and reliable part of my life, and far more than just a place to borrow books. It’s one of those spaces that quietly does everything you need without trying too hard to impress, which is part of its appeal. Located on the upper floor of the town hall building, it isn’t particularly massive or modern, but it is well-kept and comfortable. There’s a familiarity to the layout that makes it easy to settle in whether you’re there for twenty minutes or a few hours.

One of the library’s biggest strengths is its accessibility. It’s easy to get in and out, and the staff are consistently helpful and approachable without being overbearing. If you need help finding something, they’re quick to point you in the right direction, but they also give you space to browse on your own. The collection is solid for a local library. While this location may not have the depth of a larger city library, it still covers a wide range of genres, including a strong selection of fantasy and romance. Newer releases rotate in regularly, and as part of the Bergen County Cooperative Library System, you can request books from other libraries and receive them within a few days. A library card also provides access to Libby, an online e-book service with thousands of titles, which makes it easy to find something even when the physical shelves come up short.

Photo of the library from the library’s website

What stands out most, though, is how the space functions as a community hub. On any given visit, you’ll see students working and talking with friends, parents with younger kids, and older residents reading or using the computers. It rarely feels overcrowded, but it also never feels empty, maintaining a steady, low-level energy that makes it easy to focus without feeling isolated. After school hours can get a bit noisy, but it’s usually manageable, and the designated quiet section in the back is a good place to work or read without distractions. The library also runs regular events, including arts and crafts activities, movie and game nights, and book clubs for different age groups.

That said, there are a few limitations. The size of the building means seating and table space can be somewhat limited, especially during peak hours, and there aren’t many fully private areas. Additionally, while the collection is good, more niche titles sometimes require a wait or a request through another system.

Even with these drawbacks, the library remains dependable, welcoming, and easy to use. It may not be the largest or most modern facility, but as a consistent, community-centered space with a strong collection, it more than earns its place.
